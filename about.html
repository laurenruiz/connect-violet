Below are the 8 different minority women featured as different personality types. Each of them has their descriptions as well as their historical contributions to society.


Ana Roque De Duprey


Ana de Duprey was a Puerto Rican scientist, educator, and author. Duprey worked to promote education and women's suffrage in Puerto Rico, founding the first women's suffrage organization in her country. During her time she established a women's high school, a college, and a teaching academy. She also received an honorary doctorate for her achievements and aspirations. If you received Duprey as your Personality type then you are someone ambitious, strong-willed, and goal-oriented


Dr. Ellen Ochoa


Dr. Ellen Ochoa is a Hispanic-American engineer, researcher, and astronaut. She was the first Hispanic Woman to go to space as well as Johnson's space center's first Hispanic and second women director. In addition to receiving endless awards such as NASA's highest award, the Distinguished Service Medal, she's had several schools named after her and her legacy.


Mary G. Ross


Mary G Ross is the first Native American woman to be an Engineer. She was also a Founding member of Lockheed’s secret Advanced Development Program, therefore much of her work is still classified. She used her skills in order to develop military aircraft and study aerospace engineering. She is an icon in her field and will forever be remembered as the first Native American woman engineer.


Mary Jackson


Mary Jackson was Nasa’s first black female engineer. With her hard work and drive she achieved every promotion possible before her demotion in order to become a manager of both the Federal Women’s Program, in the NASA Office of Equal Opportunity Programs. While in this role, she promoted women in NASA's science, engineering, and mathematics jobs. For her hard work and efforts, she had the famous film “hidden figures” based on her legacy.


Rajeshwari Chatterjee


Rajeshwari Chatterjee was an electrical engineer and researcher. With her hard work, she was able to study abroad at The University of Michigan and receive her Ph.D. in electrical engineering. Later on, Chatterjee became the first female faculty member of the Indian Institute of Science. She also built India's first microwave engineering lab, working on studying and designing microwave circuits and systems.


Gladys West


Gladys West is an African American woman that did many works in math and computing. Her efforts were used towards the mathematical modeling of the earth as well as the development of satellite geodesy models. West worked as a programmer in the Naval Surface Warfare Center with large-scale computers, and was the second black woman to ever be hired by that Center. She also participated in an award-winning astronomical study proving the regularity of Pluto’s motion relative to Neptune. Furthermore, West was a Hokie! She earned her Ph.D. in public administration from Virginia Tech.


Lin Lanying


Lanyang is a Chinese woman who studied and got her doctorate in solid-state physics from the University of Pennsylvania and was the first Chinese student in a century to earn a doctorate there. Due to her profound skills and research, the FBI attempted to persuade her to stay in the U.S. to work but she was dedicated to her home country, China. Moving back to China, she lived a very different life. She made only $20 per month but was stuck on her goal of improving her country. In 1958, produced China’s first monocrystalline silicon, making it the third country in the world to ever produce it. The silicon is used to make most modern electronic equipment and was considered to be a huge advancement. In addition to other achievements, Lanyang was a true trooper.


Maryam Mirzakhani


Maryam Mirzakhani was a profound mathematician with a doctorate from Harvard University. She also won the quadrennial Fields Medal, which is the most prestigious award in mathematics. Mirzakhani was born in Tehran, Iran, and continued her studies in the U.S after her hard work. Mirzakhani also accepted a position as an assistant professor at Princeton University and as a research fellow at the Clay Mathematics Institute before joining the Stanford faculty. She taught as a Stanford professor until her demise. She truly never gave up on her efforts and her research is still used to date.
